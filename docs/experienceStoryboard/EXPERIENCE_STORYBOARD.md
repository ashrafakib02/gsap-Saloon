## The Cinematic Experience

> "This is not a website specification. This is a film treatment. The user is the main character. The scroll wheel is the pacing. Every frame exists for a reason."

---

## THE DIRECTOR'S NOTE

You are designing a film, not a webpage.

The visitor is the protagonist. They arrive with questions they cannot articulate — "Is this place for me? Will I be taken care of? Can I trust these people?" — and the experience must answer every one of them, not through words, but through the accumulation of feeling.

The scroll wheel is the pacing. The visitor controls the speed of the story. They can linger on a beautiful moment or rush past a less compelling one. But every moment they encounter must be worth their time. Every frame must earn its place in the edit.

This document is structured the way a film director structures a shot list: scene by scene, beat by beat, with the emotional intention behind every creative choice. The technical details serve the emotion. The emotion does not serve the technology.

**The source of truth:** PRODUCT_VISION.md, COMPETITOR_RESEARCH.md, CREATIVE_DIRECTION.md, MOODBOARD.md, DESIGN_LANGUAGE.md, VISUAL_RULES.md. This storyboard does not contradict any of them. It translates them into a narrative experience.

## THE NARRATIVE SPINE

### The Story We Are Telling

Every great film has a narrative spine — a single sentence that describes the entire story. Ours is:

**"A woman discovers a place that was made for her, and realizes she never wants to leave."**

That is the entire story. Every scene, every transition, every frame serves this narrative. The hero section is the discovery. The service sections are the evidence. The artisan section is the relationship. The booking section is the commitment. The closing is the anticipation of return.

### The Protagonist

The protagonist is not "the user" or "the visitor." She is a specific person with a specific emotional state when she arrives:

**She has been scrolling through other salon websites.** They all looked the same — crowded grids, stock photos, pop-up offers, "BOOK NOW!" buttons screaming at her. She is mildly irritated and mildly hopeless. She wants to find a place that feels right, but she is beginning to suspect that no salon website will ever feel different from any other.

Then she arrives at ours.

**Her emotional starting point:** Mild skepticism, slight fatigue, guarded openness. She has been let down before. She is ready to be impressed but expects not to be.

**Her emotional ending point:** Quiet certainty. She has found the place. She knows it in her body, not just her mind. The booking is not a decision — it is a resolution.

## THE FILM'S STRUCTURE

### Runtime

The "film" runs approximately 3-5 minutes for a first-time visitor who scrolls at a contemplative pace. This is not a 30-second TikTok. This is not a 2-hour epic. It is a short film — concise, powerful, and leaving the audience wanting more.

### Acts

The homepage follows classical dramatic structure — the same structure that has governed storytelling for 2,500 years:

```
PROLOGUE ........... The Loading Moment (3 seconds)
                         ↓
ACT I .............. THE INVITATION (45-60 seconds)
    Scene 1: Threshold ............ The Hero Moment
    Scene 2: Whisper .............. The Emotional Thesis
    Scene 3: Immersion ............ The Atmospheric World
                         ↓
ACT II ............. THE EXPERIENCE (90-120 seconds)
    Scene 4: Craft ............... Service Chapter — Hair
    Scene 5: Transformation ...... Service Chapter — Color
    Scene 6: Intimacy ............ Service Chapter — Bridal
    Scene 7: Sanctuary ........... Service Chapter — Spa
    Scene 8: The Artisans ........ The People Behind the Craft
    Scene 9: Voices .............. Social Proof & Testimonials
                         ↓
ACT III ............ THE COMMITMENT (30-45 seconds)
    Scene 10: The Invitation ..... Booking CTA
    Scene 11: The Gift ........... Gift Experience
    Scene 12: The Promise ........ Closing Moment
                         ↓
EPILOGUE ............ The Confirmation Moment (booking flow)
```

## THE SHOT DICTIONARY

Before we enter the scenes, we define the cinematic vocabulary — the "shot types" that appear throughout the experience:

| Shot Type | Digital Translation | Emotional Function |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Establishing Wide** | Full-bleed hero image, maximum viewport | Creates the world. Tells the visitor where they are. |
| **Medium Shot** | Two-column layout — image + text at moderate distance | Introduces the subject with context. Balances story and information. |
| **Close-Up** | Full-viewport image, tight crop, macro detail | Creates intimacy. Forces the visitor to notice texture, detail, craft. |
| **Extreme Close-Up** | Full-width image, microscopic detail — a hand, a tool, a product | Creates tactile sensation. The visitor feels like they can touch the image. |
| **Portrait** | Artisan/client portrait, centered or rule-of-thirds | Creates human connection. The visitor meets a person, not a brand. |
| **Tracking Shot** | Scroll-linked parallax — foreground and background at different speeds | Creates depth and movement. The visitor feels like they are moving through space. |
| **Slow Motion** | Extended animation duration (800-1200ms) for key moments | Creates emotional weight. Signals: "This moment matters. Linger here." |
| **Rack Focus** | Background blurs as foreground sharpens on scroll | Directs attention. Guides the eye from atmosphere to subject. |
| **Dissolve** | Cross-fade between two images on scroll position | Creates transformation. Before becomes after. Past becomes present. |
| **Smash Cut** | Dense section immediately following sparse section | Creates impact. The rhythm shift startles the attention back to life. |
| **Fade to Black** | Slow fade to the warm background color | Creates a chapter break. Signals: "That section is complete. Breathe." |
| **Held Shot** | Static composition with no animation | Creates stillness. Signals: "Nothing is happening. Nothing needs to happen." |

### SCENE 0: THE LOADING MOMENT

**Film Equivalent:** The studio logo before the film. The screen goes dark. A single element appears — the brand mark, or a brief atmospheric flash — then dissolves into the first frame.

**Purpose:**
Create a designed threshold between the noise of the internet and the sanctuary of the experience. This is the "door closing behind you" moment. The visitor leaves the browser tab they were in and enters our world.

**Emotion:**
Anticipation. The feeling of something about to begin. Not anxiety (which a loading spinner creates) but quiet expectation (which a designed loading moment creates).

**Story:**
The visitor has clicked a link or typed a URL. They are in transit. The loading moment is the journey from their world to ours — a brief, beautiful passage that says: "You are arriving somewhere."

**Visual Focus:**
A single atmospheric element against the warm background — a subtle particle of light, a gentle bloom of warmth, or the brand mark emerging from softness. Not a progress bar. Not a spinner. A designed moment.

**Motion Language:**
A slow, warm fade from the warm background color to the hero image. Duration: 1000-1500ms. The longest "entrance" animation in the experience — marking this as the threshold. Ease-out: the image decelerates into its final state, like a curtain settling after being drawn.

**Camera Language:**
The equivalent of the camera opening from black — the first frame of a film. The image emerges from darkness (or in our case, from warmth). The viewer is not yet "in" the scene — they are watching it materialize.

**Lighting Direction:**
From darkness to golden warmth. The image starts at 0% opacity and warms as it reveals. The light source appears to be arriving — as if someone just opened the curtains.

**Sound Imagination:**
A single, deep, resonant tone — like a singing bowl being struck once, or the sound of a heavy wooden door closing. Low frequency. Warm. Short. Then silence. The sound says: "You have arrived."

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
The hero image is now fully visible. The loading moment dissolves seamlessly into Scene 1 — there is no visible boundary between "loaded" and "first scene." The transition IS the hero reveal.

**Psychological Goal:**
Reset the visitor's attention. Break the scroll-trance of whatever they were doing before. Create a micro-pause that says: "Something different is beginning."

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I just entered somewhere special."

### SCENE 1: THE THRESHOLD — Hero Moment

**Film Equivalent:** The establishing wide shot of the film's world. The camera reveals the landscape, the city, the room — and the audience understands instantly: this is where the story takes place. Think of the first frame of "Lost in Translation" — the Tokyo skyline, intimate and overwhelming. Think of the opening of "The Grand Budapest Hotel" — the Wes Anderson symmetry that tells you everything about the world you're entering.

**Purpose:**
Stop the scroll. Create awe. Communicate the brand's entire identity in a single, wordless frame. The visitor must feel — before reading a single word — that this is not like every other salon website they have seen today.

**Emotion:**
Awe. Not "wow" (which is loud and fleeting) but awe (which is quiet and lasting). The involuntary deep breath. The moment when the eye rests and the mind goes still.

**Story:**
The protagonist has entered the world. The first image she sees IS the world — warm, beautiful, considered, and unmistakably real. There is no text to read, no button to click, no navigation to parse. Just the image. Just the feeling. Just the space.

The image must answer her unconscious question: "Is this place for me?" before she has consciously asked it. The answer must be: "Yes. This was made for someone like you."

**Visual Focus:**
One image. Full viewport. The composition is cinematic — wide enough to show the space and the atmosphere, close enough to feel intimate. The image might show:

- The salon interior in golden afternoon light — the space itself as the hero
- A client mid-service, bathed in warm light — the experience as the hero
- A detail that communicates everything — a hand on a shoulder, scissors in motion, light on brass

Whatever the image, it must carry the full weight of the brand's visual identity: warm, authentic, considered, editorial. The image IS the design. Nothing else is needed.

**Motion Language:**
The image reveals itself through the Warm Reveal — our signature interaction. It fades in from 80-90% opacity (not from black — the near-opacity means the image is almost present before the animation begins, creating the sense that it was always there and is now simply becoming clear). Duration: 800-1200ms. This is a held shot — once revealed, the image is still. No parallax, no animation, no movement. The stillness IS the statement.

**Camera Language:**
The equivalent of a locked-off wide shot — a composition so considered that the camera does not need to move. The image is framed once, framed perfectly, and held. The visitor's eye does the work — traveling through the composition, discovering details, absorbing atmosphere. The camera's job is done; the frame speaks.

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold — our signature lighting mood. The image is bathed in warm, amber-toned light. Shadows are soft and warm-toned. Highlights glow. The overall impression: "This is the most beautiful time of day, and this is where it happens."

**Sound Imagination:**
Silence. After the loading moment's single tone, the hero is pure visual silence. No ambient sound, no music suggestion, no atmospheric hum. The silence is the most powerful sound choice — it says: "Just look." The visual beauty is loud enough.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
As the visitor begins to scroll (and only then — not before), the hero image slowly fades and translates upward at a gentle parallax rate. The image doesn't disappear — it dissolves, like a scene fading in a film. Below it, the next scene's text emerges from the warm background. The transition is a slow cross-dissolve — the hero giving way to the thesis.

**Psychological Goal:**
Establish the visual standard. Communicate brand identity without language. Create the emotional baseline (awe) that all subsequent scenes will build upon or return to. Make the visitor feel that they have entered a space where every detail has been considered.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"This is different from everything else I've seen today."

### SCENE 2: THE WHISPER — Emotional Thesis

**Film Equivalent:** The title card or opening voiceover of a film — the moment where the director states their intention, not through plot, but through tone. Think of the opening text of "Her": a series of intimate letters read in voiceover. Think of the title card of "Moonlight" — simple, quiet, and carrying the weight of the entire film.

**Purpose:**
State the brand's meaning in a single, emotionally resonant sentence. Not a tagline. Not a value proposition. A thesis — a statement of belief that frames everything that follows.

**Emotion:**
Calm. The awe of the hero has settled into something quieter — a sense of recognition. The visitor reads the thesis and feels something shift internally: "Yes. That is what I have been looking for."

**Story:**
The protagonist has seen the world (Scene 1). Now she hears its voice for the first time. The thesis is spoken softly — a single headline, perhaps followed by one supporting line. It is not a sales pitch. It is not a description. It is a declaration of intent.

The thesis answers the question the hero image raised: "What IS this place?" The answer is not "a salon" — she can see that. The answer is something deeper: "This is a place where you will be taken care of."

**Visual Focus:**
Typography. Pure, beautiful typography — centered in the viewport, surrounded by generous whitespace. No image competing for attention. The words are the visual element. The typeface is our serif (voice), at Display or Heading scale, with generous line-height and letter-spacing. The composition is symmetrical, breathing, and still.

Below the headline, a single supporting line in our sans-serif (function) at Body or Subheading scale. This line elaborates — not explains — the thesis. It adds one layer of specificity without adding complexity.

**Motion Language:**
The headline reveals word by word — each word appearing with a 60-80ms delay, creating the feeling of a voice speaking slowly and deliberately. The supporting line fades in after the headline completes, with a 300ms pause. Duration total: 800-1200ms. The words are not typed (which would feel mechanical) but revealed (which feels considered).

**Camera Language:**
The equivalent of a held close-up on a character's face during a monologue — the camera is locked, the subject fills the frame, and the power comes from stillness and proximity. The typography IS the subject. The whitespace IS the composition.

**Lighting Direction:**
Even, warm, ambient — the typography is set against the warm off-white background, with no dramatic lighting effect. The light is the default state of our world — warm, calm, and present. No shadows on the text. No gradient backgrounds. Just the warmth of our surface color.

**Sound Imagination:**
A barely audible ambient tone — the kind of sound you feel more than hear. The low hum of a quiet room. The distant sound of warm air moving through a space. The visitor should not consciously register the sound — they should simply feel the atmosphere.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
As the visitor continues scrolling, the thesis text dissolves upward and the next scene — a full-bleed atmospheric image — rises from below. The transition is a gentle cross-fade: the words giving way to the world they described. The visitor moves from hearing the brand's voice to seeing its world.

**Psychological Goal:**
Create a moment of recognition — the visitor sees their own desire reflected back to them. The thesis is not telling them something new; it is naming something they already felt but couldn't articulate. This naming creates trust: "This brand understands me."

**Expected User Feeling:**
"They understand what I'm looking for."

### SCENE 3: THE IMMERSION — Atmospheric World

**Film Equivalent:** The sequence in a film where the director takes you inside the world — a slow pan across a room, a tracking shot through a hallway, a montage of details that builds the sensory reality of the setting. Think of the opening sequence of "A Single Man" — the morning ritual rendered in such exquisite detail that you feel the texture of the protagonist's life.

**Purpose:**
Place the visitor inside the salon. Not show the salon — place them IN it. The difference is emotional: showing is viewing from outside; placing is experiencing from within. This scene must create the feeling of being in the physical space — the light, the warmth, the texture, the calm.

**Emotion:**
Desire. The visitor has heard the brand's voice (Scene 2) and now sees the world it described. The desire to be in that space — to sit in that chair, to feel that light on their skin — begins to build. This is the first moment of "I want to be there."

**Story:**
The protagonist has been introduced to the world (Scene 1) and heard its intention (Scene 2). Now she explores. The immersion section is a sequence of atmospheric images — details of the space, the tools, the textures — that build a sensory portrait of the salon without a single word of copy. The images are the story. The story is: "This space is beautiful. This space was made with care. This space exists for you."

The images might include:
- Light falling through the window onto the counter
- A row of amber bottles, backlit and glowing
- Fresh towels in a ceramic bowl
- A hand reaching for a tool
- The texture of the wooden surface
- A plant leaf catching the light

Each image is a sentence in the sensory paragraph.

**Visual Focus:**
A sequence of two to four full-bleed or near-full-bleed images that create an atmospheric montage. The images vary in scale — one might be a wide view, the next a macro detail — creating visual rhythm. The composition alternates between environmental (the space) and tactile (the materials).

Between the images, brief typographic moments — a single word or short phrase in our serif typeface — act as "titles" for each image, like chapter headings in a visual poem. These words come from our brand vocabulary: "Warmth." "Touch." "Ritual." They name what the image shows without describing it.

**Motion Language:**
Each image enters with a gentle scroll-linked parallax — the background image moves at 80% of the scroll speed, creating a subtle sense of depth. The visitor feels like they are moving THROUGH the space, not past it. The parallax is subtle (10-15% differential) — felt, not seen.

The typographic words enter with a simple fade — no translation, no scale, just opacity from 0 to 1 over 400ms. The simplicity of the word's entrance contrasts with the richness of the image, creating rhythm.

**Camera Language:**
The equivalent of a Steadicam tracking shot through a space — smooth, continuous, unhurried. The camera moves through the salon at a walking pace, pausing briefly to notice details. The visitor's scroll IS the camera movement — they control the pace of the tracking shot.

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold throughout — every image in this sequence shares the same warm, amber-toned lighting. The consistency creates the feeling of a single, continuous space — the visitor moves through the salon in one golden hour that never ends.

**Sound Imagination:**
The ambient tone from Scene 2 continues, perhaps layered with very subtle texture — the faintest sound of scissors, the soft touch of fabric, the quiet hum of a space at rest. These sounds are not literal (we don't play actual scissor sounds) but atmospheric — the sonic quality of a warm, quiet room.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
The last atmospheric image dissolves into a section of breathing room — a generous expanse of the warm background with no content. This breathing space is the "fade to black" between the film's prologue (Scenes 0-3) and its first act (Scene 4). The visitor arrives at the empty space, pauses, and then scrolls into the first service chapter. The breathing space creates a chapter break — the invitation is complete; the experience begins.

**Psychological Goal:**
Create sensory immersion that bypasses rational evaluation. The visitor is no longer "looking at a website" — they are "inside a space." This shift from observer to participant is the most important psychological transformation in the entire experience. Once the visitor feels they are IN the space, every subsequent piece of information is received differently — not as marketing copy, but as a natural part of the environment they have entered.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I want to be in this space."

### SCENE 4: CRAFT — Service Chapter: Hair

**Film Equivalent:** The first major scene of Act I — where the protagonist encounters the world's first "thing of interest." In a heist film, this is the reveal of the target. In a romance, this is the meet-cute. In our film, this is the first encounter with the craft — the first moment where beauty becomes tangible and specific.

**Purpose:**
Introduce the brand's primary craft — hair — with the editorial weight it deserves. This is not a service listing. This is a short film about what it means to have your hair done by someone who has devoted their life to the craft.

**Emotion:**
Curiosity. The visitor has been immersed in atmosphere (Scene 3) — now they encounter substance. The curiosity is specific: "What does this actually look like? What can they actually do? Who does this work?"

**Story:**
The protagonist has entered the space and felt its warmth. Now she discovers what happens here. The hair chapter reveals itself as a mini-narrative: an establishing shot of the craft in action, a close-up of the technique, and a moment of revelation — the result.

The narrative follows a single service arc:
1. **The encounter** — a full-viewport image of a client and artisan in the midst of the craft
2. **The detail** — a close-up of the technique (hands, tools, hair in motion)
3. **The description** — sensory copy that makes the visitor feel the experience before booking it
4. **The proof** — a before/after or result image that validates the craft

**Visual Focus:**
The primary visual is an editorial photograph — not a service photo, but an editorial image that could appear in Kinfolk or Cereal. A stylist at work, captured in warm light, with the composition of a fashion editorial. The image communicates craft, care, and artistry simultaneously.

The supporting visual is a detail image — a macro shot of the technique. The texture of hair being cut. The color being applied. The hands of the artisan in motion. This image creates tactile connection — the visitor feels the craft through their eyes.

**Motion Language:**
The section enters with a gentle upward translation (20-30px) and opacity reveal — the standard section entry animation. The headline reveals word by word. The images use a subtle parallax differential. The overall motion is calm, considered, and unhurried — matching the pacing of the "slow opening" defined in the scroll philosophy.

**Camera Language:**
Medium shot (the establishing image) followed by close-up (the detail image). The visitor first sees the scene from a conversational distance — stylist and client in the space — then moves closer to examine the craft. The camera moves from observation to intimacy.

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold — warm, directional, creating depth. The lighting in the photography should show the actual light of the salon, capturing the quality that makes the space feel special. Side-lighting creates dimension and reveals texture in the hair.

**Sound Imagination:**
The ambient tone continues, perhaps with a very subtle layer of warmth — the sonic equivalent of the image's golden light. The sound is not louder; it is richer. The experience is deepening.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
A breathing space — 40-60% of a viewport of empty warm background. The chapter break says: "Hair is complete. The next chapter is beginning." The visitor scrolls through the breathing space and encounters the next service.

**Psychological Goal:**
Establish the craft standard. The visitor must feel that the work shown here is not merely "good" — it is the work of someone who has devoted years to mastering their technique. The editorial quality of the photography communicates: "This work deserves to be seen this way."

**Expected User Feeling:**
"These people are serious about what they do."

### SCENE 5: TRANSFORMATION — Service Chapter: Color

**Film Equivalent:** The transformation montage — the sequence in every great film where the protagonist changes. Think of the makeover scene in "My Fair Lady," but without the fanfare. Think of the quiet, devastating transformation in "Moonlight" — Chiron becoming someone new, not through spectacle but through accumulated small changes.

**Purpose:**
Tell the story of transformation — the most emotionally powerful narrative in a salon's arsenal. Color is not a service; it is a change of identity. This scene must communicate: "We don't just change your hair color. We change how you see yourself."

**Emotion:**
Desire. This is where curiosity crystallizes into wanting. The visitor sees the transformation and thinks: "I want to feel like that. I want to be that person."

**Story:**
The transformation chapter tells a specific story — not "we do color" but "here is what happens when you trust us with your color." The narrative might follow a single client's journey:

1. **Before** — a portrait of the client in her original state. Natural, real, un-styled. The visitor sees themselves.
2. **The process** — images of the color being mixed, applied, developed. The craft in motion. The artisan's hands at work.
3. **After** — the same client, transformed. Not just different hair — different posture, different expression, different energy. The transformation is emotional, not just physical.
4. **The words** — the client's own words about the experience, in her own voice. A quote that is specific, emotional, and unmistakably real.

**Visual Focus:**
The hero visual is the before/after — but NOT presented as a split screen. The before image occupies the full viewport. As the visitor scrolls through the section, the before image dissolves into the after image — a scroll-linked cross-dissolve. The visitor controls the speed of the transformation. They are the author of the reveal.

This is the **Transformation Dissolve** — our signature scroll moment from CREATIVE_DIRECTION.md. It is the most emotionally charged moment in the entire scroll experience.

**Motion Language:**
The Transformation Dissolve is the hero motion moment. The before/after cross-dissolve is scrubbed to scroll position — as the visitor scrolls, the before fades and the after emerges. The transition is slow (taking a full viewport of scroll to complete) and smooth (no jarring transitions). The visitor can pause at any point in the transformation.

Supporting motion: the process images enter with staggered reveals, creating a sense of the steps unfolding. The client quote enters with a gentle fade after the transformation completes.

**Camera Language:**
Portrait shot (before) → Detail shots (process) → Portrait shot (after). The camera mirrors the transformation arc — starting with the person, moving to the craft, returning to the person, changed.

**Lighting Direction:**
The before image uses natural, neutral light — honest, unmanipulated. The after image uses Afternoon Gold — warm, luminous, glowing. The lighting itself transforms alongside the hair. The subliminal message: "After our color, you glow."

**Sound Imagination:**
During the dissolve: a single, sustained note that slowly rises in pitch — the sonic equivalent of transformation. Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just a gentle lift, like the first note of a new morning. When the transformation completes, the note resolves and the ambient tone returns, richer than before.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
After the transformation is complete and the quote has been read, a breathing space creates the chapter break. The visitor has just experienced the emotional peak of Act II's first half. The breathing space lets the feeling settle before the next chapter.

**Psychological Goal:**
Make the transformation feel personal. The scroll-controlled dissolve puts the visitor in the driver's seat — they are not watching someone else's transformation; they are experiencing their own potential transformation. The before/after is not a comparison — it is an invitation.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I want that feeling."

### SCENE 6: INTIMACY — Service Chapter: Bridal

**Film Equivalent:** The love story within the film — the subplot that adds emotional depth and raises the stakes. Think of the quiet wedding scene in "Manchester by the Sea" — intimate, specific, and carrying more emotional weight than any action sequence.

**Purpose:**
Tell the story of the most emotionally significant service: bridal. This is not a service description — it is a love letter to the most photographed day of a woman's life. The visitor must feel the weight of the occasion and the depth of the salon's commitment to making it perfect.

**Emotion:**
Tenderness. The energy shifts from the transformation excitement of Scene 5 to something quieter, more intimate, more vulnerable. Bridal is not about looking good — it is about being seen on the day that matters most.

**Story:**
The bridal chapter is the shortest service section — and the most emotionally dense. It tells a single story: a bride on her day. Not the ceremony, not the reception — just the morning. The preparation. The moment before the moment. The hands of the stylist adjusting a veil. The bride looking in the mirror for the first time. The quiet confidence that comes from knowing you are in expert hands.

**Visual Focus:**
A single, full-viewport portrait of a bride — not in her dress, not at the altar, but in the chair. Mid-preparation. The stylist's hands visible. The light catching the details. The expression: calm, present, ready. This image communicates everything: "We understand the weight of this moment."

Supporting visuals: detail shots — the veil, the hair pins, the hands at work, the mirror reflection. These details build the sensory reality of the bridal experience.

**Motion Language:**
The slowest section in the entire scroll. Everything moves at half the usual pace — the reveal is slower, the transitions are gentler, the text appears more deliberately. The motion itself communicates: "This moment is unhurried. We take the time this occasion deserves."

**Camera Language:**
Intimate close-up — closer than any other service section. The camera is near enough to see the texture of the veil, the light in the bride's eyes, the precision of the stylist's hands. The intimacy communicates: "We are close enough to care about the details."

**Lighting Direction:**
Morning Light — the coolest of our three lighting moods, but still warm. The light of new beginnings. The light of the first hours of a day that will be remembered forever. Soft, diffused, flattering — the light of a bride's morning.

**Sound Imagination:**
Near-silence. Perhaps the faintest echo of music — a piano note, distant and reverberating. The sound of a morning that hasn't fully started yet. The sound of anticipation.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
The bridal section dissolves into a slightly denser visual rhythm — the spa chapter that follows is more sensory and more text-rich. The transition from bridal's intimacy to spa's immersion creates emotional variety — the visitor moves from tenderness to sensory pleasure.

**Psychological Goal:**
Create emotional trust. The bridal service requires the highest level of trust — the stakes are irreversible (you can redo your hair; you cannot redo your wedding photos). This section must communicate: "We understand the weight. We are worthy of your trust."

**Expected User Feeling:**
"They will take care of me on the day that matters most."

### SCENE 7: SANCTUARY — Service Chapter: Spa

**Film Equivalent:** The meditation sequence — the part of the film where the external action pauses and the internal experience takes over. Think of the spa sequence in "Call Me by Your Name" — the physical pleasure rendered with such specificity that the viewer feels it.

**Purpose:**
Complete the sensory arc. After hair (craft) and color (transformation) and bridal (intimacy), the spa chapter provides sensory immersion — the most physical, most tactile, most internal of the services.

**Emotion:**
Relief. The visitor has been processing information and emotion through the first three service chapters. The spa chapter is a release — a moment of pure sensory pleasure that lets the mind rest and the body imagine.

**Story:**
The spa chapter is the simplest service narrative: "You will be touched, and it will feel wonderful." The story is told through sensory language and tactile imagery — close-ups of treatment, textures of materials, the quality of light in a treatment room. No before/after. No transformation narrative. Just the present moment of being cared for.

**Visual Focus:**
Full-viewport images of the spa experience — but not the generic "woman with cucumber on eyes" spa photography. Instead: the texture of a warm towel. The glow of a candle reflected in oil. Hands working on skin. The quality of light in a dim room. The images evoke touch through sight.

**Motion Language:**
The motion in this section is the gentlest of any section — almost imperceptible fades, parallax so subtle it registers only as depth. The motion itself is relaxing — it slows the visitor's internal pace, creating the digital equivalent of a deep breath.

**Camera Language:**
Extreme close-up — the closest camera position in the entire experience. The camera is near enough to see the texture of skin, the grain of a towel, the movement of oil on a surface. The proximity creates tactile sensation through visual information.

**Lighting Direction:**
Evening Warmth — the deepest, warmest of our three lighting moods. The light of candles and low lamps. The light of winding down. The light of rest.

**Sound Imagination:**
The ambient tone deepens — lower, richer, more enveloping. The sonic equivalent of a weighted blanket. The sound of a room that has been specifically designed to make you feel safe and warm.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
A breathing space — but this one is longer than the others (a full viewport of empty space). The spa chapter needs more separation because it is the most internal of the service chapters. The visitor needs to emerge from the sensory experience before re-entering the external world of the artisan section.

**Psychological Goal:**
Trigger sensory memory. The visitor should feel the warmth of the towel, the pressure of the massage, the scent of the oil — through images alone. This sensory evocation creates desire that is physical, not just visual.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I can almost feel it."

### SCENE 8: THE ARTISANS — The People

**Film Equivalent:** The character introduction sequence — the moment in a film where the ensemble cast is revealed. Think of the character introductions in "The Grand Budapest Hotel" — each person given a portrait, a name, and a defining quality. Think of the team page of any great restaurant — the people are the brand.

**Purpose:**
Humanize the brand. After four service chapters (which focus on craft and experience), the artisan section reveals the people who create that experience. The visitor must feel: "These are not employees. These are artists. These are the people I will trust with my appearance."

**Emotion:**
Connection. The visitor moves from admiring the work to connecting with the people. This is the shift from "what" to "who." The emotional register changes from desire (for the service) to trust (in the person).

**Story:**
Each artisan is introduced as a portrait — a large, warm, editorial-quality image that shows the person, not just their face. The portrait communicates personality: confidence, warmth, humor, intensity, calm. Below the portrait: name, title, one sentence about their specialty, and their availability.

The narrative is not "meet our team" — it is "meet the people who will take care of you." The language shifts from brand-voice to personal-voice. Each artisan's specialty statement is specific and human: "Sarah specializes in natural-looking color — the kind that makes people ask where you got your highlights done, not what color you used."

**Visual Focus:**
Portrait photography — large, warm, editorial. Each artisan's portrait is shot in the same lighting and style, creating visual consistency while allowing individual personality to emerge. The portraits occupy the majority of the viewport — these are the most important images in the section.

The portraits use the Artisan Reveal — a signature scroll moment where the face emerges with the Warm Reveal animation, and the specialty word appears one beat later. The rhythm: face → pause → specialty. The rhythm creates intimacy — it feels like being introduced.

**Motion Language:**
The Artisan Reveal is the primary motion: the portrait fades in with the Warm Reveal (400-600ms), then the specialty text appears 200ms later. The stagger between face and text creates the rhythm of introduction — "this is [name]. She does [specialty]." The motion is identical for every artisan — consistency creates the sense of a unified team.

**Camera Language:**
Portrait shot — the classic photographic composition of a person from the chest up, positioned at rule-of-thirds, with the environment soft behind them. The portraits are the film's "hero shots" of each character — the images that would appear on the movie poster.

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold — every artisan is shot in the same warm, golden light. The consistency creates the sense that they all exist in the same space, the same time, the same world. The lighting flatters every skin tone while maintaining the brand's visual signature.

**Sound Imagination:**
The ambient tone brightens slightly — returning to the warmth of Act I after the deeper tones of the spa section. The sound of people — not voices, but the ambient presence of human beings in a room. The sonic equivalent of a room that is full of life.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
The last artisan portrait dissolves into a denser visual field — the social proof section that follows is the most information-rich moment in the entire scroll. The transition from the orderly portrait gallery to the more organic cascade of reviews creates a shift from curated presentation to authentic expression.

**Psychological Goal:**
Create parasocial connection. The visitor should feel that they already know these people — that they have been introduced, that they have formed an impression, that they could choose one of them with confidence. The portraits transform the brand from an entity into a collection of individuals.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I already know who I want to book with."

### SCENE 9: VOICES — Social Proof

**Film Equivalent:** The testimony sequence — the moment in a documentary where real people speak about their experience. Think of the interview segments in "Jiro Dreams of Sushi" — real customers describing the experience in their own words, with genuine emotion and specific detail.

**Purpose:**
Eliminate doubt. After the emotional journey of the service chapters and the personal connection of the artisan portraits, the visitor needs external validation. Not from the brand (which has been speaking) but from other visitors (who have no reason to lie).

**Emotion:**
Trust. The cumulative effect of authentic, named, specific testimonials creates the feeling: "These people are the real thing." Trust is not built by the brand saying "trust us" — it is built by other people saying "I trusted them, and it was wonderful."

**Story:**
The social proof section is the most "real" section in the entire scroll. No editorial photography. No atmospheric lighting. No cinematic motion. Just real words, real names, real faces, and real experiences. The contrast between the editorial polish of the preceding sections and the raw authenticity of this section creates a powerful effect: "All of that beauty is real — and these people can prove it."

The testimonials are organized not by category but by emotional arc — each one tells a mini-story of transformation:
1. "I was nervous about trying something new..."
2. "Sarah understood exactly what I wanted..."
3. "The moment I saw the result, I cried."
4. "I've never felt more like myself."

This emotional progression mirrors the visitor's own journey from skepticism to confidence.

**Visual Focus:**
Text-forward. This is the most text-dense section in the entire scroll — and intentionally so. The testimonials are large, readable, and given generous whitespace. Each testimonial occupies its own compositional moment — it is not crammed into a grid or a slider. The visitor reads one testimonial at a time, absorbing its story before moving to the next.

Supporting visuals: real client photographs — unposed, authentic, shot in the salon. Not headshots. Not portraits. Moments — a client laughing, a client touching her hair, a client looking in the mirror. The photographs are the visual proof that the words are true.

**Motion Language:**
The testimonials enter with a staggered cascade — each one appearing 200ms after the previous. The cascade creates the feeling of a chorus of voices — one after another, building a narrative of trust. The motion is the simplest in the entire scroll: a gentle fade-in, nothing more. The content is powerful enough that it needs no motion enhancement.

**Camera Language:**
Documentary-style — the camera is at eye level, at a natural distance, capturing the subject as they are. No editorial composition. No cinematic lighting. Just real people in real light. The contrast with the editorial photography of the preceding sections creates authenticity through visual difference.

**Lighting Direction:**
Natural light — the salon's actual light, unmanipulated. The testimonials are photographed in the same space but without the careful lighting setup of the editorial images. The slight imperfection of natural light communicates: "This is real."

**Sound Imagination:**
Multiple voices, overlapping gently — the sonic equivalent of a room full of people sharing their experiences. Not loud. Not clear. Just the warm murmur of real conversation. The sound of community.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
The last testimonial dissolves into a generous breathing space — the longest pause in the entire scroll. This pause is essential: the visitor has just absorbed a significant amount of social proof, and the emotional weight needs time to settle. The breathing space allows the trust to solidify before the booking invitation.

**Psychological Goal:**
Transfer trust from the brand to the community. The brand has made its case (Scenes 1-8). Now other people make the case. The visitor trusts these voices because they are specific, named, and unmistakably real. The social proof section transforms the visitor's internal narrative from "This brand seems good" to "These real people confirm it IS good."

**Expected User Feeling:**
"These are real people. This is real. I can trust this."

---

### SCENE 10: THE INVITATION — Booking CTA

**Film Equivalent:** The climax of the film — the moment where the protagonist makes the decision that changes everything. Think of the moment in "Before Sunrise" when Jesse asks Céline to get off the train with him. The question is simple. The weight is enormous. The answer is everything.

**Purpose:**
Convert desire into action. The visitor has been on an emotional journey — awe, calm, curiosity, trust, desire. The booking invitation is the moment where that emotional investment becomes a commitment. This scene must be the most confident, most spacious, and most frictionless moment in the entire scroll.

**Emotion:**
Confidence. The visitor is no longer uncertain. She knows what she wants. She knows who she wants. She knows this is the place. The booking invitation does not create desire — it resolves it.

**Story:**
The story is simple: "When you're ready, we're here." That is the entire narrative of this section. After the density of the social proof section, the booking section is strikingly simple — a single headline, a single statement, a single action.

The headline is warm and confident: "Your chair is waiting." or "When you're ready." or "Let's begin."
The statement is specific and inviting: "Choose your service, choose your artisan, choose your moment."
The action is the booking CTA — our Primary button, in our accent gold, with the label "Book your experience."

**Visual Focus:**
Radical simplicity. The entire section is typography and a button. No image. No illustration. No decoration. The whitespace is the visual element — the most generous margin in the entire scroll. The visitor's eye goes directly to the headline, then to the CTA. The path from desire to action is a straight line.

**Motion Language:**
The booking section enters with stillness — no animation, no reveal, no motion. It is simply there, as if it has been waiting. The lack of motion is intentional: this section has nothing to prove, nothing to reveal, nothing to convince. It exists. The CTA is available. The invitation stands.

**Camera Language:**
The equivalent of a held wide shot after a dramatic climax — the camera pulls back to show the full space, and the protagonist stands in the center, alone, at the moment of decision. The simplicity of the composition communicates: "Everything you need is here."

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold — the warmest, most beautiful lighting. The booking section should feel like the golden hour of the entire experience — the moment when everything is bathed in the most beautiful light.

**Sound Imagination:**
Silence. Pure silence. After all the ambient tones and atmospheric sounds, the booking section is sonically empty. The silence says: "Nothing else matters right now. Just this decision."

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
If the visitor does not book (which is fine — many won't), they scroll past the booking section into the gift section. The transition is a gentle descent — the confidence of the booking section giving way to a secondary invitation. The gift section is quieter, less prominent, but equally considered.

If the visitor DOES book, the transition is immediate — the booking flow slides in from the edge, a designed overlay that maintains the brand experience. The visitor never leaves the world.

**Psychological Goal:**
Remove the final barrier. The visitor has all the information she needs. The booking section does not add information — it removes friction. The simplicity of the section says: "There is nothing else to consider. When you are ready, we are here."

**Expected User Feeling:**
"Yes. This is it."

---

### SCENE 11: THE GIFT — Gift Experience

**Film Equivalent:** The subplot resolution — the secondary story that adds warmth and depth without competing with the main narrative. Think of the gift-giving subplot in "Amélie" — a small, beautiful gesture that reveals character.

**Purpose:**
Introduce the gift experience for visitors who are not booking for themselves. The gift section is quieter than the booking section — a gentle suggestion rather than a direct invitation. It serves the tertiary audience (gift buyers) while maintaining the scroll's emotional rhythm.

**Emotion:**
Warmth. The gift section evokes the pleasure of giving — the anticipation of someone else's joy. The visitor who is not booking for herself may be inspired to book for someone she loves.

**Story:**
The gift section is brief — a single compositional moment. A warm image (perhaps a gift card in beautiful packaging, or a pair of hands receiving a gift), a headline ("Give the gift of being taken care of"), and a CTA ("Explore gift options"). The story is simple: "The best gifts are experiences."

**Visual Focus:**
A single image and a single headline. The image is warm, intimate, and specific — not a generic "gift box with ribbon" but a real moment of giving or receiving. The composition is simple and symmetrical — a moment of balance after the asymmetry of the preceding sections.

**Motion Language:**
The gift section enters with the standard section reveal — a gentle upward translation and fade. The motion is identical to other content sections, maintaining consistency. The section does not demand attention — it waits to be discovered.

**Camera Language:**
Medium shot — the classic distance of interpersonal interaction. The camera is at the distance of a conversation, close enough to feel connection, far enough to feel comfortable.

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold — warm, inviting, and consistent with the rest of the experience. The gift section does not have its own lighting mood — it exists within the same warm world as everything else.

**Sound Imagination:**
The ambient tone returns — gently, at low volume. The silence of the booking section gives way to the warmth of continued experience. The visitor is still in the world.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
The gift section dissolves into the final scene — the closing moment. The transition is a gentle fade, with the gift content disappearing as the closing image emerges. The narrative arc is nearing its end.

**Psychological Goal:**
Serve the secondary audience without disrupting the primary narrative. The gift section is always present but never prominent — it is a quiet door that is always open, for visitors who enter through it.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I could give this to someone I love."

---

### SCENE 12: THE PROMISE — Closing Moment

**Film Equivalent:** The final shot of the film — the image that stays with the audience after the credits roll. Think of the final shot of "Lost in Translation" — the whisper, the embrace, the ambiguity. Think of the final frame of "Her" — the city skyline, vast and full of possibility. The final image must be as carefully considered as the first, because the Peak-End Rule tells us: people remember the beginning and the end.

**Purpose:**
Create the final emotional impression. The closing moment is the last thing the visitor sees before leaving (or booking). It must encapsulate the brand's entire emotional identity in a single, lingering image.

**Emotion:**
Longing. The visitor has completed the scroll. She has seen the world, met the people, read the stories, and been invited to book. The closing moment creates the feeling of not wanting to leave — the desire to scroll back up, to return, to be in this world again.

**Story:**
The story is circular — we return to the beginning. The closing image echoes the hero image (Scene 1) but from a different perspective — perhaps the same space, different time of day. Perhaps the same person, different angle. Perhaps the same light, different season. The circularity communicates: "This world exists. It is always here. You can return whenever you want."

The closing text is a single line — a promise, a welcome, a statement of permanence. Something like: "We'll be here." or "Your place is kept." or simply the brand name, alone, centered, in our serif typeface.

**Visual Focus:**
A single image — full viewport, full bleed, maximum impact. The image is the most atmospheric, most beautiful, most emotionally resonant in the entire scroll. This is the image that must be worthy of the Peak-End Rule.

The composition is the inverse of the hero: where the hero was an establishing wide shot (world), the close is an intimate detail (feeling). Perhaps a single object — a cup of tea, a warm towel, a shaft of light on a surface — that contains the entire brand's identity in one quiet frame.

**Motion Language:**
The closing image reveals with our longest, slowest animation — a 2000-2500ms dissolve from the warm background to the image. The duration is deliberate: this is the slowest moment in the entire scroll, the moment that lingers longest, the moment that creates the lasting impression. The image doesn't appear — it arrives. Slowly. Considered. Unhurried.

**Camera Language:**
The equivalent of a slow zoom-out — the camera gradually reveals more of the scene, expanding from a detail to the full composition. But in our case, the zoom is emotional, not physical — the image fades in, and as it becomes clearer, the visitor's emotional understanding deepens. They see more not because the camera moves, but because they are paying more attention.

**Lighting Direction:**
Afternoon Gold at its warmest and most beautiful — the golden hour of the golden hour. The light is at its most amber, its most glowing, its most transformative. This is the light that makes everything look its most beautiful — and it is the light the visitor carries with them when they leave.

**Sound Imagination:**
A single, warm, sustained tone — the same tone from the loading moment, but now it carries the weight of the entire experience. It is no longer a single note of anticipation; it is a chord of completion. The sound resolves. The film is over. The visitor is changed.

**Transition Into Next Scene:**
There is no next scene in the scroll. The experience ends here. If the visitor scrolls further, they encounter the footer — the practical, unglamorous, necessary information (contact details, hours, address, social links). The footer is the "credits" of the film — functional, complete, and respectful of the experience that preceded it.

**Psychological Goal:**
Create the lasting impression. The Peak-End Rule ensures that the visitor will remember this moment more than any other. The closing image must be so beautiful, so warm, and so considered that it creates the emotional equivalent of a return ticket — the desire to come back.

**Expected User Feeling:**
"I don't want to leave."

## THE SUPPORTING PAGES

The homepage is the film. The supporting pages are the extended universe — they deepen the story for visitors who want to know more.

### SERVICE DETAIL PAGES

**Film Equivalent:** The director's commentary — a deeper dive into a specific scene.

**Purpose:** Allow the visitor to explore a specific service in full detail — the complete sensory description, the full artisan connection, the transparent pricing, and the direct booking path.

**Emotion:** Deepening. The visitor who clicks into a service page is moving from browsing to investigating. The emotion shifts from general interest to specific intent.

**Cinematic Approach:** Each service page is a three-act mini-film:
1. **The Invitation** — A full-viewport editorial image with the service name. The emotional thesis of this specific service.
2. **The Experience** — A scroll-narrative that describes the service through sensory language, process imagery, and artisan expertise. The visitor "experiences" the service before booking it.
3. **The Resolution** — Transparent pricing, artisan selection, and embedded booking. The conversion path is clear, frictionless, and within the brand experience.

**Key Difference from Homepage:** The service page is slower, denser, and more detailed. The homepage gives a taste; the service page gives the full meal. The pacing is more unhurried, the descriptions more sensory, the photography more intimate.

### ABOUT / THE SPACE PAGE

**Film Equivalent:** The location reveal — the scene in a film where the full scope of the world is shown.

**Purpose:** Tell the story of the salon itself — its philosophy, its space, its history, its people. The About page is for visitors who have been emotionally converted by the homepage and now want to understand the intellectual foundation.

**Emotion:** Respect. The visitor has fallen in love with the experience; the About page earns their respect by revealing the thinking, the craft, and the values behind it.

**Cinematic Approach:** The About page is the most editorial of all pages — it reads like a magazine feature. Photography of the space (environmental, warm, detailed), photography of the team (editorial, human, specific), and text that tells the brand's story with the same warmth and specificity as the rest of the experience.

**Key Difference from Homepage:** The About page is the only page where the brand speaks about itself at length. Everywhere else, the brand speaks about the visitor's experience. Here, the brand shares its own story — but always in service of the visitor's understanding, never in service of the brand's ego.

## THE RHYTHM MAP

### Pacing Across the Full Experience

The pacing curve of the homepage follows the scroll philosophy defined in PRODUCT_VISION.md, visualized as a film's tempo:

```
Scene:        Loading → Hero → Whisper → Immersion → Services → Artisans → Proof → Booking → Gift → Close
              ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tempo:        Presto → Largo → Adagio → Andante → Moderato → Andante → Allegro → Largo → Adagio → Largo
              (fast)  (slow)  (slow)   (walking)  (moderate)  (walking) (brisk)  (slow)  (slow)  (slow)
              ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Visual:       Minimal → Full → Text → Images → Mixed → Portraits → Dense → Minimal → Image → Full
              ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Emotion:      Anticipation → Awe → Calm → Desire → Curiosity → Connection → Trust → Decision → Warmth → Longing
```

### The Breathing Pattern

Every scene transition includes a breathing space. The breathing spaces are not empty — they are designed moments of rest that allow the previous scene's emotion to settle before the next scene begins.

| Transition | Breathing Duration | Purpose |
|------------|-------------------|---------|
| Loading → Hero | None (seamless) | The threshold IS the hero reveal |
| Hero → Whisper | Minimal (cross-dissolve) | The awe flows directly into the thesis |
| Whisper → Immersion | Minimal (cross-dissolve) | The words dissolve into the world |
| Immersion → Services | Full viewport | Chapter break — prologue complete, Act II begins |
| Between services | 40-60% viewport | Chapter breaks between service narratives |
| Services → Artisans | 40-60% viewport | Shift from craft to people |
| Artisans → Proof | 40-60% viewport | Shift from curation to authenticity |
| Proof → Booking | Full viewport | The longest pause — trust needs time to settle |
| Booking → Gift | Minimal | Secondary invitation follows primary naturally |
| Gift → Close | Minimal | The gift dissolves into the promise |
| Close → Footer | Minimal | The film ends; the credits begin |

## THE DIRECTOR'S CHECKLIST

Before any scene is approved, it must pass this test:

1. **Does this scene advance the emotional journey?** If the scene does not move the visitor closer to the booking decision (or the emotional state that leads to it), the scene is unnecessary.
2. **Does this scene contain one idea?** If the scene tries to say two things, it says nothing. Split it.
3. **Does this scene have a clear visual hierarchy?** The visitor's eye should land on one element first, then travel to the next in a defined path. If the eye bounces, the composition is broken.
4. **Does this scene breathe?** After this scene, does the visitor need a moment to process? If yes, the next transition must include a breathing space.
5. **Does this scene's motion serve the content?** If you removed the animation, would the content still be powerful? If no, the animation is doing too much work.
6. **Does this scene's lighting match the mood?** The lighting must serve the emotional intention — not just be "pretty."
7. **Would this scene make sense in complete silence?** The visual and narrative power must be sufficient without any sound, music, or audio suggestion. Sound enhances; it never substitutes.
8. **Does this scene respect the visitor's time?** If the visitor gave this scene 10 seconds of attention, would they leave feeling that those 10 seconds were well-spent?

## THE FINAL DIRECTOR'S NOTE

This storyboard is a living document. It describes the ideal experience — the version of the website that exists in its most considered, most polished, most emotionally resonant form.

The implementation will not achieve this ideal on the first pass. Photography must be shot. Copy must be written. Motion must be crafted. Spacing must be calibrated. Each scene will go through iterations — and with each iteration, the team should return to this storyboard and ask: "Does our scene serve the emotional intention? Does it advance the narrative? Does it create the feeling we intended?"

The storyboard is the north star. The implementation is the journey. The journey may take detours, but the destination is always the storyboard's vision.

**The one thing to remember:**

The visitor is the main character. Every creative decision — every image, every word, every animation, every pixel — exists to serve her journey. If a creative choice serves the designer's ego, the developer's convenience, or the stakeholder's anxiety instead of the visitor's experience, the choice is wrong.

She is the main character. The scroll is the pacing. The story is hers.
