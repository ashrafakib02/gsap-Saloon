# EMOTIONAL_TIMELINE.md
## The Feeling at Every Step

> "The visitor arrives with a question she cannot articulate. Every emotional state we create is an answer to that question — delivered not through words, but through the accumulation of visual, spatial, and kinetic sensation. The timeline is the emotional contract we keep with her from first pixel to final click."

---

## DOCUMENT PURPOSE

This document maps every emotional state the visitor moves through across the complete experience — from the first millisecond of page load to the final moment of booking confirmation. For each state, it defines:

- **The desired emotion** — what she should feel, precisely
- **Why it matters** — what that feeling does to her decision-making
- **What visual elements reinforce it** — the compositional, typographic, and photographic choices that create the feeling
- **What motion reinforces it** — the timing, easing, and choreography that sustain the feeling
- **What should never happen** — the specific failure that would break the emotional contract

This is not an animation spec. This is not a style guide. This is the emotional blueprint — the feeling we are building, step by step, pixel by pixel, moment by moment.

**Relationship to other documents:**
- EXPERIENCE_STORYBOARD.md → The cinematic structure (what happens, scene by scene)
- SCROLL_STORY.md → The narrative pacing (how time moves through the scroll)
- CREATIVE_DIRECTION.md → The artistic vision (what it should feel like)
- DESIGN_LANGUAGE.md → The compositional grammar (how we compose)
- VISUAL_RULES.md → The constitutional rules (what we must and must not do)
- **EMOTIONAL_TIMELINE.md → The feeling at every step (what the visitor experiences)**

## 1. PAGE LOAD — The Threshold

### Desired Emotion: Anticipation

Not anxiety. Not impatience. Anticipation — the quiet, expectant feeling of something about to begin. The feeling of standing at the entrance of a beautiful room, the door just having closed behind you, the light just beginning to reveal itself. She has arrived somewhere, and something is about to unfold.

### Why It Matters

The page load is the first emotional contract. If the loading moment creates anxiety (a spinner, a blank screen, a generic progress bar), the contract is broken before the story begins. The visitor's guard goes up. The skepticism she carried from other salon websites is confirmed: "This is just another website."

If the loading moment creates anticipation, the guard drops — just slightly, just enough. She leans forward. She expects something. That expectation is the emotional foundation upon which everything else is built.

The loading moment determines whether she enters the experience as a participant or as an observer. Participant is the only acceptable outcome.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **A single atmospheric element against the warm background.** Not a spinner. Not a progress bar. A designed moment — a subtle particle of light, a gentle bloom of warmth, or the brand mark emerging from softness. The element is the only thing on screen. Its singularity communicates: "This moment has been considered."
- **The warm background color itself.** Before anything else appears, the background color is the first visual signal. It is warm, cream-toned, alive — the digital equivalent of walking into a room where the light is already golden. The color says: "You are entering warmth."
- **Nothing else.** No navigation. No text. No promotional banners. No cookie consent. The absence of everything except the atmospheric element and the warm background is the most powerful visual choice. The emptiness communicates: "We are not in a hurry. We have been expecting you."

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **A slow, warm fade from the warm background color.** Duration: 1000-1500ms. The longest entrance animation in the entire experience — marking this as the threshold, the passage from the outside world to ours. The animation is time-linked (not scroll-linked) because the visitor has not yet begun to scroll — this is the only moment where we control the pacing.
- **Ease-out curve.** The element decelerates into its final state, like a curtain settling after being drawn. The deceleration communicates weight, presence, and deliberateness. Nothing snaps. Nothing bounces. The element arrives with the physical weight of a well-made object being placed on a wooden surface.
- **No other motion.** The atmospheric element is the only moving thing. Its isolation creates focus — the visitor's eye goes to the single point of change, and nowhere else. The stillness around the motion amplifies the motion's significance.

### What Should Never Happen

- A generic loading spinner. Spinners communicate "this website is loading like every other website." Our loading moment communicates "you are arriving somewhere special."
- A blank white screen. Even a millisecond of pure white before content appears breaks the warmth contract. The warm background must be the very first thing the eye encounters.
- A progress bar. Progress bars create expectation of duration — "I have to wait X seconds." Our loading moment creates expectation of experience — "Something beautiful is about to begin."
- Multiple animated elements. Two things moving at once splits attention and creates visual noise. One element, one motion, one feeling.
- Any text that says "Loading..." or "Please wait." These words break the fourth wall. The visitor should never be reminded that they are using a website. They should feel like they are entering a space.

## 2. HERO — The First Impression

### Desired Emotion: Awe

Not "wow" — which is loud, fleeting, and forgettable. Awe — which is quiet, lasting, and transformative. The involuntary deep breath. The moment when the eye rests and the mind goes still. The feeling of walking into a five-star hotel lobby and having the world slow down around you.

Awe is the most important emotion in the entire experience. It is the emotional baseline that all subsequent emotions build upon or return to. If the hero does not create awe, the entire narrative arc is compromised — because every emotion that follows is measured against this first impression.

### Why It Matters

The hero section answers the visitor's unconscious question: "Is this place for me?" before she has consciously asked it. The answer must be: "Yes. This was made for someone like you."

Awe accomplishes three things simultaneously:
1. **It stops the scroll.** The visitor was scrolling through other salon websites. She stops. The image demands her attention — not through volume, but through beauty.
2. **It establishes the visual standard.** Every subsequent element is measured against the hero. If the hero is extraordinary, everything that follows benefits from the halo effect. If the hero is mediocre, nothing that follows can compensate.
3. **It communicates brand identity without language.** The hero image IS the brand. Warm, authentic, considered, editorial. The visitor understands everything about the brand's visual identity before reading a single word.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **One image. Full viewport.** The image occupies the entire screen. There is no navigation competing for attention. No headline. No button. No text. The image is the composition, and the composition is the message.
- **Cinematic composition.** The image is framed like a film still — wide enough to show the space and atmosphere, close enough to feel intimate. The composition follows the rule of thirds, with generous negative space that communicates restraint and confidence.
- **Afternoon Gold lighting.** The image is bathed in warm, amber-toned light — the golden hour that is our signature lighting mood. Shadows are soft and warm-toned. Highlights glow. The overall impression: "This is the most beautiful time of day, and this is where it happens."
- **Editorial photography.** The image could appear in Kinfolk or Cereal — art-directed, authentic, composed with the same care as a magazine spread. This is not a service photo. This is not a stock image. This is a considered composition that communicates craft, warmth, and authenticity simultaneously.
- **No text.** The most powerful typographic choice in the hero is the absence of text. The image speaks. The visitor listens. Words would diminish the image's power — they would shift the mode from visual absorption to linguistic processing. The hero is visual only.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **The Warm Reveal — our signature interaction.** The image fades in from 80-90% opacity, not from black. The near-opacity means the image is almost present before the animation begins — the reveal is a gentle clarification, not a dramatic appearance. The visitor feels the image was always there and is now simply becoming visible, like a room brightening as curtains are drawn.
- **Duration: 800-1200ms.** This is the longest "entrance" animation in the experience after the loading moment. The duration marks the hero as significant — this is not a casual content reveal; this is the establishing shot of the film.
- **Ease-out curve.** The image decelerates into its final state. The deceleration communicates the weight of a heavy curtain being drawn, or the slow brightening of a room as dawn arrives.
- **Stillness after reveal.** Once the image is fully visible, it holds. No parallax. No animation. No movement. The stillness IS the statement. The image is so considered, so composed, so beautiful that it does not need to move. The visitor's eye does the work — traveling through the composition, discovering details, absorbing atmosphere.

### What Should Never Happen

- A carousel or slider. One image. One hero. One first impression. A carousel communicates indecision — "We can't decide which image is best, so we'll show you all of them." A single image communicates confidence — "This is our best. We're showing it once."
- Text overlaid on the image. Text over a busy photographic background destroys legibility and diminishes the image's power. The hero is image-only. Text has its place in the next scene.
- Auto-playing video. The visitor controls the experience. Auto-playing video is the most hostile thing a website can do — it hijacks bandwidth, attention, and control. A still image communicates confidence. Video communicates desperation.
- A zoom or Ken Burns effect. The image is still. Movement on the hero image would distract from the composition and create motion sickness on long exposures. The composition is the point, not the motion.
- Any element that appears before the image. The image is the first content the visitor encounters. Nothing precedes it. Nothing competes with it. The hero is the threshold, and the image is the door.

## 3. STORY — The Emotional Thesis

### Desired Emotion: Calm

The awe of the hero has settled into something quieter — a sense of recognition. The visitor reads the thesis and feels something shift internally: "Yes. That is what I have been looking for." The calm is not passive. It is the calm of someone who has been searching and has just found the first clue — a quiet certainty that this place understands her.

### Why It Matters

The story section (the Emotional Thesis) states the brand's meaning in a single, emotionally resonant sentence. It is not a tagline. Not a value proposition. A thesis — a statement of belief that frames everything that follows.

Calm matters because the visitor has been emotionally activated by the hero (awe) and now needs to orient. She needs to understand what she is feeling before she can invest further. The calm of the thesis section gives her a moment to register the brand's voice — to hear, for the first time, how this place speaks.

The thesis also creates the first moment of textual engagement. The visitor has been in visual-only mode (hero). Now she reads. The transition from seeing to reading must be gentle — the typography must be as beautiful as the image that preceded it, or the mode-switch will feel jarring.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **Typography as the primary visual element.** A single headline, centered in the viewport, surrounded by generous whitespace. No image competing for attention. The words are the visual element — set in our serif typeface at Display or Heading scale, with generous line-height and letter-spacing. The composition is symmetrical, breathing, and still.
- **Generous whitespace.** The headline floats in warmth — 70% or more of the viewport is empty. The whitespace communicates: "These words have been chosen. They are important. They deserve space."
- **The warm background.** No gradient. No texture. No decorative treatment. The warm off-white background is the canvas, and the typography is the art. The simplicity of the background communicates: "Nothing else needs to be here."
- **A single supporting line.** Below the headline, a single line in our sans-serif typeface at Body or Subheading scale. This line elaborates — not explains — the thesis. It adds one layer of specificity without adding complexity.
- **Center alignment.** The headline and supporting line are centered. Center alignment communicates formality, importance, and singular focus. It says: "This is the main thing. This is our voice."

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **Word-by-word reveal.** The headline reveals word by word — each word appearing with a 60-80ms delay, creating the feeling of a voice speaking slowly and deliberately. The reveal is not typed (which would feel mechanical) but revealed (which feels considered). Each word materializes from the warm background, as if the voice is materializing from silence.
- **Supporting line fade.** After the headline completes, a 300ms pause, then the supporting line fades in over 400ms. The pause creates rhythm — the headline speaks, there is a breath, the supporting line elaborates. The timing mirrors natural speech: statement, pause, elaboration.
- **Total duration: 800-1200ms.** The complete reveal — headline plus supporting line — takes just over a second. Long enough to feel deliberate, short enough to not impede the scroll.
- **Stillness after reveal.** Once all text is visible, it holds. No further animation. The stillness lets the words land — the visitor reads, absorbs, and internalizes the thesis before scrolling onward.

### What Should Never Happen

- A typing effect on the headline. Typing effects feel mechanical and digital — the opposite of our warm, considered brand voice. Words are revealed, not typed. The difference is the difference between a person speaking and a machine printing.
- An image alongside the thesis. The thesis is typographic. Adding an image would split attention and dilute the thesis's power. The words are enough. The whitespace is enough.
- Multiple sentences or paragraphs. The thesis is one headline, one supporting line. If it takes more than two lines to state the brand's belief, the belief is not clear enough. Editing is the proof of clarity.
- Center-aligned body copy. The supporting line may be centered (it is short, headline-length). But any body-length text below must be left-aligned for readability. Center alignment works for declarations; left alignment works for narratives.
- Animated text effects beyond the word-by-word reveal. No text that scales, rotates, bounces, or uses spring physics. The reveal is the only motion. Everything else would be decoration.

## 4. ATMOSPHERE — The Immersion

### Desired Emotion: Desire

The visitor has heard the brand's voice (thesis) and now sees the world it described. The desire to be in that space — to sit in that chair, to feel that light on her skin, to be surrounded by that warmth — begins to build. This is the first moment of "I want to be there." Not "I want to book." Not "I want to buy." I want to be in that physical space, right now, in that light.

### Why It Matters

Desire is the emotional engine that drives the rest of the experience. Without desire, the service chapters are information. With desire, the service chapters are evidence. The visitor is no longer evaluating — she is imagining herself in the space. This shift from observer to participant is the most important psychological transformation in the entire experience.

The atmosphere section creates desire through sensory immersion — images of the space, the tools, the textures, the light. No words. No descriptions. No explanations. Just the sensory reality of the salon, communicated through photography that evokes touch through sight.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **A sequence of atmospheric images.** Two to four full-bleed or near-full-bleed images that create an atmospheric montage: light falling through a window onto the counter, amber bottles backlit and glowing, fresh towels in a ceramic bowl, a hand reaching for a tool. Each image is a sentence in the sensory paragraph.
- **Variation in scale.** The images vary from wide environmental views to macro details — creating visual rhythm. The alternation between environmental (the space) and tactile (the materials) builds a complete sensory portrait.
- **Consistent Afternoon Gold lighting.** Every image shares the same warm, amber-toned lighting. The consistency creates the feeling of a single, continuous space — the visitor moves through the salon in one golden hour that never ends.
- **Brief typographic moments.** Between images, single words or short phrases in our serif typeface — "Warmth." "Touch." "Ritual." — act as chapter headings in a visual poem. These words name what the image shows without describing it.
- **Editorial photography quality.** The images are composed like a Kinfolk spread — considered, authentic, and atmospheric. The photography quality communicates: "This space is worthy of this level of attention."

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **Scroll-linked parallax.** Each image moves at 80% of the scroll speed, creating a subtle sense of depth. The visitor feels like she is moving THROUGH the space, not past it. The parallax is subtle (10-15% differential) — felt, not seen.
- **Simple word fades.** The typographic words enter with a plain opacity fade — no translation, no scale, just opacity from 0 to 1 over 400ms. The simplicity of the word's entrance contrasts with the richness of the image, creating rhythm.
- **Walking pace.** The section moves at an "andante" tempo — the pace of someone walking through a gallery, pausing at what catches their eye. The scroll rewards a moderate pace. Fast scrolling through the atmosphere section is a design failure.
- **No section-reveal animation.** The images are already present in the scroll — they reveal through parallax, not through entry animations. The continuous parallax creates the feeling of a tracking shot, not a series of individual reveals.

### What Should Never Happen

- Stock photography. Every image must be original. Stock images communicate "we don't care enough to photograph our own space." The atmosphere section is entirely dependent on photography quality. One stock image would destroy the immersion.
- Descriptive copy. "Our salon features natural light, premium products, and a relaxing atmosphere." This kind of description reduces a sensory experience to a spec sheet. The images speak. The words (when they appear) are poetic fragments, not descriptions.
- A grid of images. A grid is a catalog. The atmosphere section is a journey — images appear one at a time as the visitor scrolls, each given its full viewport moment. The sequential reveal creates narrative; the grid creates inventory.
- Fast parallax. More than 15-20% differential induces dizziness and breaks the immersive spell. The parallax should be felt as depth, not seen as motion.
- Any call-to-action. The atmosphere section is not the place for conversion. It is the place for desire. A CTA here would interrupt the sensory immersion with a transactional demand. The visitor is imagining herself in the space — do not pull her out of that想象.

## 5. SERVICES — The Craft

### Desired Emotion: Curiosity

The visitor has been immersed in atmosphere (desire) — now she encounters substance. The curiosity is specific: "What does this actually look like? What can they actually do? Who does this work?" The curiosity is not idle — it is desire seeking evidence. She wants to believe this place is as good as it looks, and the services chapter provides the proof.

### Why It Matters

Curiosity is the bridge between emotional desire and rational evaluation. The visitor has felt something (awe, calm, desire) — now she needs to understand what she felt. The services chapter provides the intellectual substance that supports the emotional investment. Without this substance, the emotion fades. With it, the emotion deepens into conviction.

The services chapter is also where the visitor begins to self-select. She sees the hair work, the color transformations, the bridal preparation, the spa treatments — and she begins to imagine which of these is hers. The curiosity becomes personal: "Which of these services is the one I need?"

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **Editorial photography of craft in action.** Not service photos — editorial images that could appear in Kinfolk. A stylist at work, captured in warm light, with the composition of a fashion editorial. The image communicates craft, care, and artistry simultaneously.
- **Close-up detail images.** Macro shots of the technique — texture of hair being cut, color being applied, hands of the artisan in motion. These images create tactile connection — the visitor feels the craft through her eyes.
- **Sensory copy.** Service descriptions are narratives, not bullet-point lists. The copy makes the visitor feel the experience before booking it. "90 minutes of undivided attention, a custom consultation, and color mixed while you watch."
- **Transparent pricing.** Every service has a visible price — presented as a fact, not a selling point. The transparency communicates: "We have nothing to hide. Our value speaks for itself."
- **Artisan connection.** Each service chapter includes the name and face of the artisan who performs the work. The visitor begins to form parasocial connections with specific people.
- **Breathing spaces between services.** Each service chapter is separated by 40-60% viewport of empty warm background. The breathing creates chapter breaks — the visitor processes one service before encountering the next.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **Standard section reveal.** Each service section enters with a gentle upward translation (20-30px) and opacity reveal — the standard section entry animation (400-600ms). The consistency creates rhythm — the visitor learns the pattern and feels the predictability as reliability.
- **Word-by-word headline reveal.** The service name reveals word by word, creating a moment of focused attention on the service title. The reveal is brief (200-300ms total) — enough to mark the moment, not enough to slow the scroll.
- **Subtle parallax on images.** Background images move at 80% of scroll speed, maintaining the depth sensation established in the atmosphere section.
- **Moderate pace.** The services chapter moves at a "moderato" tempo — faster than the atmosphere section (the visitor is now exploring, not lingering) but slower than the social proof section (she is still processing, not scanning).

### What Should Never Happen

- Bullet-point service lists. "Cut & Style — $XX. Color — $XX. Blowout — $XX." This format reduces a rich experience to a menu board. Our service descriptions are narratives that communicate the experience, not inventories that communicate the price.
- Generic service photography. "Woman in salon chair" is not editorial photography. Every image must be composed, lit, and art-directed with the same care as the hero image. The service photography IS the brand.
- Pricing hidden behind "inquire" or "contact us." Transparency is a trust signal. Hidden pricing is an insecurity signal. Every service has a visible price.
- Service descriptions that could appear on any salon website. "We offer a range of hair services." This is content without specificity. Every description must be specific to this brand, this salon, this experience.
- Auto-advancing service carousels. Each service gets its own section, its own scroll moment, its own chapter. Carousels hide content and create uncertainty. Our services are presented with editorial courage — one at a time, each given its full attention.

## 6. TRANSFORMATION — The Mirror Moment

### Desired Emotion: Longing

This is where curiosity crystallizes into wanting. The visitor sees the transformation — the before, the process, the after — and thinks: "I want to feel like that. I want to be that person." The longing is not for the service (a haircut, a color). The longing is for the feeling the service provides — the confidence, the radiance, the transformation of self-image.

### Why It Matters

Transformation is the most emotionally powerful narrative a salon can tell. It is the story of becoming someone new — not through spectacle, but through accumulated small changes. The transformation section is the emotional peak of Act II — the moment where desire reaches its highest intensity.

The scroll-controlled dissolve puts the visitor in the driver's seat — she is not watching someone else's transformation; she is experiencing her own potential transformation. The before/after is not a comparison — it is an invitation. The longing that results is the most motivated emotional state in the entire experience — it is the state that makes booking feel inevitable.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **The Transformation Dissolve — our signature scroll moment.** A full-viewport section that presents a before-and-after transformation through a scroll-controlled cross-dissolve. The before image occupies the viewport. As the visitor scrolls, the before fades and the after emerges. The visitor controls the speed of the transformation.
- **Before image: natural, honest lighting.** The before portrait uses neutral, unmanipulated light. The subject is natural, real, un-styled. The visitor sees herself.
- **After image: Afternoon Gold lighting.** The after portrait uses warm, luminous, glowing light. The lighting itself transforms alongside the hair. The subliminal message: "After our color, you glow."
- **The subject's expression changes.** The before image shows someone waiting. The after image shows someone who has arrived — different posture, different expression, different energy. The transformation is emotional, not just physical.
- **A client quote.** Below the transformation, a single quote in the client's own words — specific, emotional, and unmistakably real. The quote names the feeling the visitor is experiencing.
- **Process images.** Supporting images of the color being mixed, applied, and developed — the craft in motion. These images bridge the before and after, creating narrative continuity.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **Scroll-linked cross-dissolve.** The before/after transition is scrubbed to scroll position — as the visitor scrolls, the before fades and the after emerges. The transition takes a full viewport of scroll to complete. The visitor can pause at any point in the transformation. She can scroll back to see the before again. She is the author of the reveal.
- **Staggered process images.** The process images enter with staggered reveals (200ms between each), creating a sense of the steps unfolding. The rhythm mirrors the actual process: mix, apply, develop, reveal.
- **Client quote fade.** The quote enters with a gentle fade after the transformation completes — a moment of stillness after the motion of the dissolve. The quote arrives like a voice speaking after a long silence.
- **Slow tempo.** The transformation section moves at an "andante" to "largo" pace — slower than the service chapters, demanding more of the visitor's time. The slowness communicates: "This moment matters. Linger here."

### What Should Never Happen

- A split-screen before/after. Side-by-side comparisons feel clinical — like a dermatology ad. The scroll-controlled dissolve is editorial, cinematic, and personal. The visitor controls the reveal; she is not shown a comparison.
- Multiple transformations in sequence. One transformation, one story, one emotional arc. Multiple transformations would create a slideshow — efficient but emotionally thin. One transformation, deeply felt, is worth more than five transformations, quickly scanned.
- A slider control. The visitor scrolls; she does not drag a slider. The scroll IS the control mechanism. A slider would add a UI element that breaks the cinematic immersion.
- Speed or urgency. "See the transformation!" or any language that frames the before/after as a spectacle. The transformation is intimate, not spectacular. The pacing is unhurried. The visitor takes her time.
- An image that doesn't show a face. The transformation is about a person, not about hair. The visitor must see the face — the before expression, the after expression, the human story. Hair without a face is a product shot. Hair with a face is a transformation.

## 7. ARTISTS — The People

### Desired Emotion: Connection

The visitor moves from admiring the work to connecting with the people. This is the shift from "what" to "who." The emotional register changes from desire (for the service) to trust (in the person). The visitor sees the artisans — not as employees, not as service providers, but as artists. She begins to form a parasocial relationship: "I already know who I want to book with."

### Why It Matters

Connection is the bridge between desire and trust. The visitor has seen the work (desire) and now meets the people who create it (connection). This meeting transforms the brand from an entity into a collection of individuals. The visitor is no longer trusting a company — she is trusting a person.

Connection also serves a practical function: it pre-qualifies the booking decision. By the time the visitor reaches the booking section, she has already chosen her artisan in her mind. The booking flow simply confirms what the emotional journey has already decided.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **Large, warm, editorial portraits.** Each artisan's portrait occupies the majority of the viewport. The portraits are shot in the same lighting and style (Afternoon Gold), creating visual consistency while allowing individual personality to emerge.
- **Portrait composition.** The portraits use the classic photographic composition — chest up, rule-of-thirds, environment soft behind them. These are the "hero shots" of each character — the images that would appear on the movie poster.
- **The Artisan Reveal — our signature scroll moment.** The portrait fades in with the Warm Reveal animation, then the specialty text appears 200ms later. The rhythm: face → pause → specialty. The rhythm creates intimacy — it feels like being introduced.
- **Personal language.** Each artisan's specialty statement is specific and human: "Sarah specializes in natural-looking color — the kind that makes people ask where you got your highlights done." The language shifts from brand-voice to personal-voice.
- **Visual consistency.** Every artisan is shot in the same lighting, same style, same composition. The consistency creates the sense that they all exist in the same space, the same time, the same world — a unified team.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **The Artisan Reveal.** The portrait fades in with the Warm Reveal (400-600ms), then the specialty text appears 200ms later. The stagger between face and text creates the rhythm of introduction. The motion is identical for every artisan — consistency creates the sense of a unified team.
- **Stillness after reveal.** Once the portrait and text are visible, they hold. The artisan is present. The stillness communicates dignity — these are people, not content to be scrolled past.
- **Medium pace.** The artisan section moves at an "andante" tempo — the pace of walking through a gallery of portraits. Each portrait gets its moment. The scroll speed is self-directed — the visitor lingers on the artisan that draws her.
- **No hover effects on portraits.** The portraits are still. They do not zoom, scale, or warm on hover. The portrait is a person, not a card. The lack of interaction communicates: "This is a human being. Treat them with the respect of stillness."

### What Should Never Happen

- Small thumbnails in a grid. "Meet our team" grid layouts reduce people to cards. Our artisans are introduced one at a time, each given the full weight of a portrait. The grid communicates a roster. The portrait gallery communicates an ensemble cast.
- Stock photography or AI-generated faces. Every artisan portrait must be a real photograph of the actual person. Stock or AI faces destroy the parasocial connection instantly — the visitor senses the artificiality.
- Corporate bios. "John has 15 years of experience and is certified in..." Corporate language creates distance. Personal language creates connection. "John sees every head of hair as a canvas — his signature is the kind of cut that looks effortless but takes precision."
- Back-of-head photography. We see faces. We see expressions. We see eyes. The back of a head is anonymous. A face is a person.
- Animated or bouncing entry effects. The artisans arrive with dignity. Gentle fades. Considered timing. No spectacle. These are people being introduced, not content being revealed.

## 8. TESTIMONIALS — The Proof

### Desired Emotion: Trust

The cumulative effect of authentic, named, specific testimonials creates the feeling: "These people are the real thing." Trust is not built by the brand saying "trust us" — it is built by other people saying "I trusted them, and it was wonderful." The trust that results is not the brand's trust (which is self-serving) but the community's trust (which is irrefutable).

### Why It Matters

Trust is the final emotional barrier before booking. The visitor has experienced desire (atmosphere, services, transformation) and connection (artisans) — but she still needs external validation. Not from the brand (which has been speaking) but from other visitors (who have no reason to lie).

The testimonials section is the most "real" section in the entire scroll. No editorial photography. No atmospheric lighting. No cinematic motion. Just real words, real names, real faces, and real experiences. The contrast between the editorial polish of the preceding sections and the raw authenticity of this section creates a powerful effect: "All of that beauty is real — and these people can prove it."

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **Text-forward composition.** This is the most text-dense section in the entire scroll — and intentionally so. The testimonials are large, readable, and given generous whitespace. Each testimonial occupies its own compositional moment.
- **Named and attributed.** Every testimonial has a real name, a real date, and a real service. No anonymous reviews. No "— Sarah, verified client." The specificity is the proof: "This is a real person who really went there."
- **Real client photography.** Supporting visuals are unposed, authentic client moments — a client laughing, touching her hair, looking in the mirror. Not headshots. Not portraits. Moments. The photographs are the visual proof that the words are true.
- **Natural lighting.** The client photos use the salon's actual light — unmanipulated. The slight imperfection of natural light communicates: "This is real."
- **Emotional arc in testimonials.** The testimonials are organized not by category but by emotional progression: nervous → understood → transformed → confident. This progression mirrors the visitor's own journey.
- **Staggered cascade.** The testimonials enter in a staggered cascade — each one appearing 200ms after the previous. The cascade creates the feeling of a chorus of voices.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **Simple fade-in.** The testimonials enter with a gentle opacity fade — the simplest motion in the entire scroll. The content is powerful enough that it needs no motion enhancement. The fade communicates: "Here are some voices. Listen."
- **Staggered timing.** Each testimonial appears 200ms after the previous. The stagger creates the rhythm of multiple people speaking — one after another, building a narrative of trust.
- **No parallax.** The testimonials are text. Text should not parallax. The absence of motion effects on text communicates: "Read this. It matters."
- **Medium-fast pace.** The testimonials section moves at an "allegretto" tempo — the fastest section in the scroll. The visitor reads quickly through the testimonials, absorbing the chorus of voices. The pace mirrors how we naturally consume social proof — scanning names, reading key phrases, accumulating conviction.

### What Should Never Happen

- Anonymous testimonials. "A happy client" or "— Verified Buyer." Anonymity destroys trust. Every review has a name, a date, a service, and a story.
- Testimonials without specificity. "Great salon! Highly recommend!" Generic praise is indistinguishable from fabrication. Specific praise is irrefutable: "I was nervous about trying balayage for the first time, but Sarah understood exactly the tone I wanted."
- Star ratings without context. Five stars without a name, a story, or a date is meaningless. Our social proof is narrative, not numerical.
- A carousel of reviews. The testimonials are stacked vertically, each given its full moment. A carousel would hide content and create uncertainty. Our testimonials are presented with editorial courage — one at a time, fully visible.
- Testimonial photography that looks like headshots. The client photos must be candid moments — in the salon, in the chair, touching their hair. Posed headshots look like staff photos, not client photos.

## 9. BOOKING — The Invitation

### Desired Emotion: Confidence

The visitor is no longer uncertain. She knows what she wants. She knows who she wants. She knows this is the place. The booking invitation does not create desire — it resolves it. The confidence is quiet and internal — the feeling of someone who has made a decision and is ready to act on it.

### Why It Matters

Confidence is the emotional state that converts browsing into booking. The visitor has been on an emotional journey — awe, calm, desire, curiosity, longing, connection, trust. The booking section is the moment where that emotional investment becomes a commitment.

Confidence matters because it removes the final barrier. The visitor has all the information she needs. The booking section does not add information — it removes friction. The simplicity of the section says: "There is nothing else to consider. When you are ready, we are here."

The booking section must be the most confident, most spacious, and most frictionless moment in the entire scroll. After the density of the social proof section, the booking section is strikingly simple — a single headline, a single statement, a single action. The contrast is the confidence.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **Radical simplicity.** The entire section is typography and a button. No image. No illustration. No decoration. The whitespace is the visual element — the most generous margin in the entire scroll. The visitor's eye goes directly to the headline, then to the CTA.
- **The headline.** Warm and confident: "Your chair is waiting." or "When you're ready." or "Let's begin." The headline is in our serif typeface at Display scale, centered, surrounded by vast whitespace. The typography communicates confidence through proportion — the headline is large enough to command attention, surrounded by enough space to breathe.
- **The CTA.** Our Primary button, in our accent gold, with the label "Book your experience." The button is the most prominent interactive element on the page — the gold accent draws the eye, the generous sizing invites the tap. But it is not aggressive. It is not animated. It is not screaming. It is available.
- **The booking statement.** Below the headline, a single line: "Choose your service, choose your artisan, choose your moment." The statement reframes the booking as a choice, not a transaction. The visitor is not filling out a form — she is choosing her experience.
- **Afternoon Gold lighting mood.** The booking section should feel like the golden hour of the entire experience — the moment when everything is bathed in the most beautiful light.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **Stillness.** The booking section enters with no animation at all. No reveal. No fade. No translation. It is simply there, as if it has been waiting. The lack of motion is intentional: this section has nothing to prove, nothing to reveal, nothing to convince. It exists. The CTA is available. The invitation stands.
- **The longest breathing space precedes it.** A full viewport of empty warm background separates the testimonials from the booking section. The pause is essential: the visitor has absorbed significant social proof, and the emotional weight needs time to settle. The breathing space allows the trust to solidify into confidence before the booking invitation appears.
- **CTA hover state.** When the visitor hovers over the booking button, the gold accent shifts in luminosity — a gentle warmth that communicates: "I see you. I am here. I am ready when you are." The hover state is 200ms, subtle, considered.
- **CTA click state.** When the visitor clicks, the button compresses slightly (97% scale) and returns — the physical weight of a quality button being pressed. The click state confirms the action was registered. The booking flow begins.

### What Should Never Happen

- Countdown timers. "Limited availability!" "Only 2 spots left today!" Urgency is the opposite of our brand. We attract through quality. We never chase through pressure.
- A pop-up or modal. The booking flow is embedded, not overlaid. If the visitor books, the flow slides in gently from the edge — a designed overlay that maintains the brand experience. The visitor never leaves the world.
- A complex form. The booking flow is a conversation, not a form. Each step is a considered question, not a field to be filled. The flow feels like the beginning of a relationship, not the end of a transaction.
- Aggressive animation on the CTA. No pulsing. No glowing. No bouncing. No "look at me!" The CTA is confident, visible, and understated. It communicates availability, not desperation.
- A different visual treatment. The booking flow uses the same typography, same colors, same spacing, same photography as the rest of the experience. The transition from browsing to booking is seamless. The visitor never feels the seam.

## 10. CONFIRMATION — The Resolution

### Desired Emotion: Completion

The booking is confirmed. The journey is complete. The visitor has moved from skepticism to certainty, from browsing to committing, from observer to participant. The completion is not just transactional (the booking is made) — it is emotional (the story has resolved). The visitor feels satisfied, warm, and anticipatory. She is looking forward to something.

### Why It Matters

The confirmation is the final emotional beat of the booking journey — and, by the Peak-End Rule, one of the two moments the visitor will remember most (the other being the hero). A weak confirmation — a generic "Thank you for your booking" — would undermine the entire emotional investment. The confirmation must honor the commitment with the same warmth, consideration, and beauty that defined every preceding moment.

The confirmation also creates the bridge between digital and physical. The visitor has been in the website's world. Now she will be in the salon's world. The confirmation is the last thing she sees before that transition — it must leave her feeling excited, not relieved. She should feel the anticipation of an experience she is looking forward to, not the satisfaction of a task she has completed.

### What Visual Elements Reinforce It

- **A warm welcome, not a receipt.** The confirmation language is personal: "We've reserved your [Service Name] with [Artisan Name] on [Date] at [Time]." Below: preparation guidance — what to do before the appointment, what to bring, what to expect. Below: a personal touch — "We're looking forward to seeing you."
- **The brand's visual identity.** The confirmation uses the same typography, same colors, same spacing as the rest of the experience. It is not a transactional screen — it is a continuation of the brand experience. The visitor is still in our world.
- **Generous whitespace.** The confirmation is spacious. The information is clear, organized, and surrounded by breathing room. The spaciousness communicates: "There is no rush. Take your time reading this. We are happy you are coming."
- **A single atmospheric image.** Perhaps the closing image from the scroll — a warm, intimate detail that encapsulates the brand experience. The image creates the last visual impression before the visitor leaves the website.
- **Next steps.** Clear, specific, warm guidance: what to expect, how to prepare, when to arrive. The guidance eliminates anxiety and builds anticipation.

### What Motion Reinforce It

- **The Warm Confirmation — our signature interaction.** The confirmation message emerges with the Warm Reveal animation — but in slow motion (1000-1200ms). This extended duration marks the moment as special, a quiet celebration of commitment. The checkmark (if present) draws itself in the brand's gold accent, accompanied by a gentle warm fade.
- **The longest confirmation animation in the experience.** At 1000-1200ms, this is the most generous animation timing in the booking journey. The duration communicates: "This moment matters. We are honoring your commitment with the most considered motion in our vocabulary."
- **Stillness after confirmation.** Once the confirmation message is visible, it holds. The visitor reads, absorbs, and internalizes. The stillness communicates: "You have arrived. You are welcome here."
- **A gentle, sustained tone.** The sonic equivalent of the Warm Confirmation — a single, warm, sustained tone that resolves the ambient soundscape. The tone says: "The journey is complete. You are where you belong."

### What Should Never Happen

- Confetti or celebration animations. Confetti says "you did something hard!" Our booking flow says "this was effortless." The confirmation is calm, beautiful, and satisfying — not a party.
- A generic "Thank you" without personalization. "Thank you for your booking!" is transactional. "We've reserved your color consultation with Sarah on Thursday at 2pm" is relational. The confirmation must include the specific details of what was booked.
- An upsell. "While you're here, would you like to add a treatment?" The confirmation is a moment of resolution, not an opportunity for additional sales. The visitor has committed. Honor that commitment. Do not extend it.
- A redirect to a third-party platform. The confirmation exists within our world. No redirects to Fresha, no external booking platforms, no "you will be redirected in 5 seconds." The visitor stays in our brand experience from first pixel to final confirmation.
- A complex receipt layout. The confirmation is not a receipt. It is a welcome. The language is warm, the layout is spacious, and the feeling is anticipatory — not transactional.

## THE EMOTIONAL TRANSITIONS

### How One State Becomes the Next

The emotional states do not exist in isolation. They flow into each other through designed transitions — breathing spaces, cross-fades, and pacing shifts that calibrate the emotional handoff between moments.

|------------|-----------|-----------|----------|

| Story → Atmosphere | Calm → Desire | Cross-dissolve (text fades, image rises) | 400-600ms |

## THE PEAK-END RULE APPLICATION

### The Two Moments That Define Memory

Psychology tells us people judge experiences by their most intense moment and their ending. For our experience, these two moments are:

**Peak: The Transformation Dissolve (Scene 5)**
The most emotionally intense moment in the scroll. The scroll-controlled before/after cross-dissolve where the visitor becomes the author of the transformation. This moment must be so beautiful, so personal, and so emotionally resonant that it becomes the thing the visitor remembers and tells others about.

**End: The Closing Image (Scene 12)**
The final visual impression. The longest, slowest animation in the entire scroll (2000-2500ms). The image that stays with the visitor after she leaves. This moment must be as carefully considered as the hero — because the Peak-End Rule tells us: people remember the beginning and the end. The closing image must create the lasting impression — the emotional equivalent of a return ticket.

**Together, these two moments — the transformation and the close — define the entire experience's memory.** Every other moment supports, builds toward, or recedes from these two peaks. If they succeed, the experience succeeds. If they fail, no amount of excellence in other sections can compensate.

## THE FINAL DIRECTOR'S NOTE

The emotional timeline is not a formula. It is a contract — a promise that every moment the visitor spends in our experience will be met with a designed emotion that serves her journey. The timeline is the feeling we are building, and every pixel, every animation, every word must be measured against it.

When a design element does not serve the emotional timeline, it is removed. When a design element contradicts the emotional timeline, it is corrected. When a design element enhances the emotional timeline, it is preserved.

The visitor is the main character. The emotion is the story. The timeline is her journey.

**The one thing to remember:**

Every emotional state exists to bring the visitor one step closer to the quiet certainty: "This is the place. These are my people. This is my moment." If any emotional state does not advance that certainty, it is the wrong emotion, in the wrong place, at the wrong time.

Get the emotion right, and the booking follows. Get the emotion wrong, and no design can save it.
